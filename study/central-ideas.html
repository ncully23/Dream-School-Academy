Core Strategy: Last Sentence First

  • Skip to the last sentence of the passage or paragraph.
  • This often contains the main idea, author’s final claim, or conclusion.
  • Most useful for:
    • Main idea questions
    • Author’s purpose questions
    • Support for a claim/conclusion
    • “The text most strongly suggests...” questions

Time-Saving Benefits

  • Saves 30+ seconds per question
  • Great for early questions (5–10)
  • Frees time for later inference or science questions

How to Apply This Strategy

  • Read the question first — identify what it’s asking.
  • Jump to the last sentence of the passage or paragraph.
  • Look for:
    • Transition words (e.g., however, ultimately, therefore)
    • Synonyms of terms from question or answers
    • Degree words (e.g., strongly, barely, sufficiently)
  • Eliminate answers that contradict the last sentence.
  • Optionally check the sentence before the last for context.

Extra Tips

  • The first sentence also helps — often introduces the main topic.
  • Look for repetition of ideas, keywords, and transitions.
  • Paraphrase tough vocabulary — don’t get stuck.
  • Be okay with partial understanding — don’t overthink.
  • Practice pattern recognition to train this instinct.

Answer Choice Elimination Tactics

  • Avoid choices that are:
    • Too broad or vague
    • Too specific or focused on a detail
    • Contradict the text
    • Add new or unmentioned information
    • Use extreme words (always, never)

What to Focus on in the Text

  • First and last sentence of each paragraph
  • Proper nouns, dates, or places (especially in science/history)
  • Descriptive language in literary passages (emotions, traits)
  • Cause-effect or contrast relationships
  • Sentences that include: “this suggests,” “this implies,” “this highlights”

Recognize SAT Answer Choice Patterns

  • One choice is clearly supported by the text
  • One is too abstract or vague
  • One twists a detail or adds an assumption
  • One sounds right but has no textual support (trap)

Common Text Relationships to Watch For

  • Contrast: however, but, although
  • Agreement/build: in addition, furthermore, also
  • Cause and effect: therefore, because, as a result

Final Reminders

  • Approach like a lawyer — don’t guess, prove it from the text.
  • Don’t rely on your own memory — find evidence.
  • Stick to what is said, not overly inferred.
