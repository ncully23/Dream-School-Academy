What Inference Passages Usually Contain

  • Observations or Data: Multiple findings that suggest a logical next step or conclusion.
  • Contrasts or Surprises: Unexpected results often lead to correct inferences.
  • Cause-Effect Gaps: You may need to infer the missing link or result.
  • Missing Conclusions: Look for signals like “suggests that,” “therefore,” or “hence.”
  • Assumption Checks: Understand logical implications, human behavior, or scientific method.

Common Inference Answer Types

  • Confirmation: Reinforces unexpected results.
  • Generalization: Broad takeaway from examples.
  • Recommendation: Logical action based on findings.
  • Prediction: Forecasting based on trend or data shift.
  • Refutation or Complication: Disputes or adds nuance to accepted belief.

What to Eliminate in Wrong Answers

  • Extreme claims (e.g., “always,” “never”).
  • Outside knowledge or assumptions not found in the text.
  • Overly specific or narrow facts.
  • Emotional language when the passage is neutral.
  • Reversed logic or cause/effect inversion.

How to Approach Inference Questions

  • Ask: “If all this is true, what must logically follow?”
  • Watch for surprising results that require takeaways.
  • Map evidence to conclusion (or vice versa).
  • Track subtle comparisons, reversals, or implications.

Category Examples

  • Scientific Study: Unexpected result → New ecological/behavioral insight
  • Historical: Forgotten records → Reconsideration of historical value
  • Literary: Character/author detail → Rethinking their role
  • Behavioral/Economic: Study behavior → Recommendation for business or policy
  • Data-Driven: Inconsistency in data → New interpretation or classification

Inference Examples

  • Logging Study: Limited logging may help forests → Conservation should be reevaluated.
  • Zelda Fitzgerald: She was a writer → Viewing her only as a muse is incomplete.
  • Work Interruptions: Social interruptions help morale → Not all interruptions should be banned.
  • Sweet Potatoes: Genetics show earlier divergence → Natural dispersal or earlier human travel implied.
  • Biodiversity Counts: Variability in microorganism data → Definitions are subjective.
