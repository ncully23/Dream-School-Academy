🔗 Transition Types and Their Functions

  • Additions Also, Additionally, Moreover
    • Adds a new supporting point to the argument.
  • Continuations Furthermore, In addition
    • Continues or extends a prior point without shifting focus.
  • Comparisons Likewise, Similarly
    • Highlights similarity or parallel between ideas.
  • Examples For example, For instance
    • Introduces a specific case to illustrate a broader point.
  • Specific Emphasis Specifically, In particular
    • Highlights a narrowed or especially relevant detail.
  • Clarifications or Rephrasings In other words, That is, That is to say
    • Restate the idea in simpler terms.
  • Summative Conclusions Overall, In conclusion, To summarize
    • Conclude or encapsulate ideas discussed.
  • Contrasts However, On the other hand, In contrast
    • Highlight opposition or differences between ideas.
  • Concessions Although, Even though, While
    • Admit a point or acknowledge a counterpoint.
  • Sequence First, Next, Then, Finally
    • Indicate a step-by-step progression of ideas.
  • Emphasis Indeed, In fact, Certainly
    • Reinforce an important point in new words.
  • Context-Based Transitions
    NeverthelessEven with that said, the point still stands.
    RatherThe alternative is preferred or more accurate.
    InsteadSomething replaces what came before.
    StillDespite what's been said, this remains true.
