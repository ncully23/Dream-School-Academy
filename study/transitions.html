📘 Transition Types and Their Functions

  • Additions
    Add a new related idea to the previous statement.
    • also
    • furthermore
    • moreover
    • too
    • besides
  • Continuations
    Maintain the same line of thought.
    • in addition
    • additionally
    • on top of that
  • Comparisons
    Highlight similarity between two ideas.
    • likewise
    • similarly
    • in the same way
  • Examples
    Provide a specific example to support the prior point.
    • for example
    • for instance
    • such as
    • in particular
  • Specific Emphasis
    Call attention to a particularly important detail.
    • especially
    • notably
    • above all
  • Clarifications or Rephrasings
    Restate the idea in simpler terms.
    • in other words
    • that is
    • to put it differently
  • Summative Conclusions
    Conclude or encapsulate ideas discussed.
    • in conclusion
    • overall
    • to sum up
    • ultimately
  • Contrasts
    Highlight a difference or opposition between ideas.
    • however
    • on the other hand
    • nevertheless
    • in contrast
  • Concessions
    Acknowledge a point while maintaining a different stance.
    • although
    • though
    • even though
    • while
  • Sequence
    Show chronological or logical progression.
    • first
    • next
    • then
    • finally
  • Emphasis
    Reinforce an important point in new words.
    • indeed
    • in fact
    • certainly
  • Context-Based Transitions
    Communicate a specific relationship or shift.
    • meanwhile – at the same time as something else
    • instead – in place of what was expected
    • therefore – as a result
    • otherwise – if not; or else
    • consequently – as a logical result
