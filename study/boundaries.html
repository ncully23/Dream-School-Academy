📏 SAT Boundaries: How to Join and Separate Clauses

  • Joining Two Independent Clauses Use semicolon, period, or comma + conjunction
    • Correct: "She left early; she was tired." or "She left early. She was tired."
    • Incorrect: "She left early, she was tired." (comma splice)
  • Main Clause + Supplementary Phrase Use comma, colon, or dash as needed
    • Correct: "The ship took six days to dislodge, in part due to its size."
    • Correct: "Three items—New Granada, Venezuela, and Quito—were liberated."
  • Complex Lists with Internal Commas Use semicolons to separate items
    • Correct: "He used dried leaves; man-made trash, such as plastic bags; and glue."
  • Colons Used after a full sentence to introduce a list or explanation
    • Correct: "She had one goal: win the prize."
    • Avoid using a colon directly after a verb or preposition unless the sentence is complete.
  • Dashes Used for emphasis or interruption
    • Correct: "He brought three things—passion, grit, and talent—to every project."
    • Dashes can replace parentheses but must match.
  • Common Mistakes Avoid these error types
    • Comma splice: Joining two full sentences with just a comma.
    • Run-on: No punctuation or conjunction between clauses.
    • Mismatch in complex series: Using commas and semicolons inconsistently.
    • Incorrect boundary marker: Using a semicolon before a dependent clause.
  • Tips for Success Before choosing an answer...
    • Find and label all clauses (independent vs. dependent).
    • Decide if you're joining two full thoughts or adding extra detail.
    • Match punctuation styles throughout the sentence.
    • If unsure, try reading aloud — pauses often match boundaries.
