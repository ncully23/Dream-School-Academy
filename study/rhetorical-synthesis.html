🧠 Rhetorical Synthesis: Key Strategies

  • Understand the Goal “Present,” “introduce,” “emphasize,” or “describe”
    • Match the exact rhetorical purpose stated in the question.
  • Include What Matters Use info that directly supports the stated goal
    • The correct answer often combines more than one bullet point.
  • Primary Aim of a Study Why did the researchers do this?
    • Look for the motivation or research question, not just results.
  • Introduce a Study or Book Author + subject + purpose
    • Skip background if audience is already familiar with the topic.
  • Describe a Work or Object Describe features + who made it
    • Focus on visual details and link them to the creator.
  • Explain an Advantage or Disadvantage State the benefit or drawback + why it matters
    • Show the impact of a trait (e.g., digital access = easier availability).
  • Highlight Similarities or Differences Must mention both sides + a clear comparison
    • Emphasize shared traits (or contrast) clearly and evenly.
  • Emphasize a Specific Feature Such as distance, quantity, or method
    • The best answer states the feature explicitly (e.g., “sixty-two miles”).
  • Describe How Something is Used Function and context
    • Explain how a material or feature is integrated into a process or object.
  • Common Wrong Answers Recognize and eliminate these
    • Too general or vague (no clear focus)
    • Too narrow (misses the broader rhetorical goal)
    • Wrong focus (gives facts but misses intent)
    • Mismatch to audience (e.g., defines familiar terms)
  • Time-Saving Tip Underline the rhetorical goal first
    • Quickly rule out any choice that doesn’t directly match the question’s ask.
